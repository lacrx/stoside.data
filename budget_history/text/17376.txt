BACKGROUND AND ANALYSIS
On June 5, 2024, the City Council adopted the FY 2024-25 budgets. The approved General Fund
includes $216.28 million in revenues, with recurring expenditures totaling $215.45 million and an
additional $3.26 million allocated for one-time costs, bringing the total General Fund budget to
$218.71 million. Across all funds, the City adopted a budget of $594.18 million in revenues and
$604.56 million in expenditures.
This report summarizes the preliminary results of the General Fund and other major operating funds
for the fiscal year ending June 30, 2025. It compares budget and actual results for revenues and
expenditures and also provides financial status updates for the City’s Enterprise Funds, including
Water Utilities and Harbor. The report is preliminary and unaudited; final reports will be presented in
December 2025 following completion of the City’s annual audit.
Actual revenues for the year totaled $234.3 million, while actual expenditures reached $233.8 million,
resulting in a preliminary surplus of $0.5 million. Expenditures exceeded the originally adopted
General Fund budget primarily due to midyear budget amendments approved by Council. These
amendments increased total appropriations by $31.5 million, including $2.4 million carried forward
from the prior year, $22.0 million in Council-approved adjustments, $2.3 million in mutual aid
expenditures, $2.7 million for CalPERS unfunded liability payments, and $1.0 million for employee
separation payouts. Major Council-approved expenditure adjustments included $8.25 million
allocated to the El Corazon Park Site 1 project using prior year surplus, $3.0 million for the SiFi and
Ubiquity projects (to be reimbursed), $2.7 million for the Oceanside Boulevard undergrounding utility
project, $2.3 million for the San Luis Rey River project, and $2.1 million for the Crown Heights
Resource Center rehabilitation project, among others (see Quarterly Financial Report attachment for
full detail).

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY 
This preliminary report summarizes the financial activity of the City’s major operating funds for the fiscal 
year ending June 30, 2025. It is not intended to capture all finance and accounting transactions, but 
rather to provide the City Council and the public with an overview of the City's general fiscal condition. 
Final audited results will be presented in December 2025. 
 
As of June 30, 2025, the City of Oceanside remains in a stable financial position. General Fund revenues 
continue to perform well, with strong performance in property tax. 
 
 
GENERAL FUND 
The General Fund is the City’s primary operating fund, supporting essential municipal services such as 
public safety, parks and recreation, library services, and general government operations. It is largely 
funded by local taxes, including property tax, sales tax, and transient occupancy tax (TOT). 
 
On June 5, 2024, the City Council adopted a General Fund budget of $216.28 million in revenues, 
$215.45 million in recurring expenditures, and $3.26 million in one-time costs, for a total expenditure 
budget of $218.71 million.  
 
Amendments during the year added $7.3 million in revenues, consisting of $3.5 million in Council-
approved adjustments, $1.0 million in investment earnings, $2.2 million in mutual aid reimbursements, 
and $0.6 million in various other changes.  The largest Council-approved revenue adjustments include 
$3.0 million related to the SiFi and Ubiquity projects and $0.3 million for the Harbor’s design and bid 
package for the remote utility metering system implementation.  
 
Expenditure amendments totaled $31.5 million, including $2.4 million carried forward from the prior year, 
$22.0 million in Council-approved adjustments, $2.3 million in mutual aid expenditures, $2.7 million for 
CalPERS unfunded liability payments, $1.0 million for employee separation payouts, and $1.1 million in 
other adjustments and true-ups.  Major Council-approved expenditure adjustments include $8.25 million 
allocated to the El Corazon Park Site 1 project using prior year surplus, $3.0 million for the SiFi and 
Ubiquity projects, $2.7 million for the Oceanside Boulevard undergrounding utility project, $2.3 million 
for the Army Corps of Engineers, $2.05 million for the Crown Heights Community Resource Center 
Building Rehabilitation project, $0.9 million for demolition related to the pier fire, $0.5 million for cannabis 
support and auditing services, $0.3 million for building plan review and permit technician services, $0.3 
million for the Harbor remote metering system, and $0.2 million for a Cal OES payment. 
 
Actual revenues for the year totaled $234.3 million, while actual expenditures reached $233.8 million, 
resulting in a preliminary surplus of $0.5 million. These figures are unaudited; final audited results will 
be presented in December 2025.

GENERAL FUND REVENUES 
By June 30, General Fund revenues reached $234.3 million, approximately 105 percent of the amended 
budget, an overall increase of 4 percent compared to the prior year. 
 
 
 
Property Taxes  
Property taxes remain the City’s largest revenue source, accounting for 39 percent of total General Fund 
revenues. Through June 30, property tax revenues reached 103 percent of the amended budget, 
reflecting a 6 percent increase compared to the prior fiscal year. This growth is primarily driven by 
increases in assessed property values and continued new construction activity. 
 
The median sale price for detached single-family homes in Oceanside was $875,000 in the second 
quarter of 2025, compared to $905,000 in the same period of the prior year, a 3 percent decrease. This 
change reflects the mix of homes sold during the year, rather than a decline in overall home values. 
Oceanside has a broad range of home prices, so fluctuations in which types of properties sell in a given 
period can influence the median price without indicating a shift in the underlying market.

Sales Taxes  
Sales taxes represent 13 percent of total General Fund revenues. Through year-end, sales tax revenues 
reached 107 percent of the amended budget, finishing the year 1 percent below the prior year’s actual 
revenues. This result aligns with the City’s forecast, which anticipated slower growth in FY 2024–25. 
 
While most major categories, including construction, fuel, and general consumer goods, experienced 
declines, the restaurant and auto sectors saw modest gains, which helped offset some of the decreases 
in other areas. The positive variance from budget is largely due to the City’s conservative revenue 
forecasting, which anticipated these trends and positioned the budget accordingly. 
 
GENERAL FUND EXPENDITURES 
 
General Fund expenditures reached 93 percent of the amended budget as of June 30, 2025. Actual 
spending totaled $233.8 million, about $32.15 million, or 16 percent, higher than the prior year.  
 
In addition, the City recorded an unrealized investment gain of $6.2 million due to year-end market 
valuation of the investment portfolio. This non-cash accounting adjustment does not impact operating 
service levels but is required under generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP).

ENTERPRISE FUND 
Enterprise funds, also known as business-type activities, are used to account for the acquisition, 
operation and maintenance of governmental facilities and services that are entirely or predominantly 
self-supporting through user charges.  These funds are structured to reflect operations in a manner 
similar to private enterprises, showing a net profit or loss. The financial status of operating funds for 
Water, Sewer and Solid Waste Disposal are discussed in this section. Water and Sewer budgets consist 
of multiple funds such as operating, replacement and developer connection. Debt service is not 
included. Generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP) call for fiscal analysis of Enterprise Funds 
to reflect a roll up of all funds into one report. 
 
WATER FUND 
Overall revenues for the Combined Water Funds are at 111 percent of the amended budget as of June 
30. A primary reason for this increase in revenue is due to greater than expected water sales. Delinquent 
accounts and receivable balances have decreased compared to the prior year. Staff is reaching out to 
the delinquent customers offering reasonable payment plans to help them become current.  The 
following table highlights the amount and percentage expended by each water program.  As of June 30, 
the Water Funds expended 98 percent of the amended operating budget.  Operating revenues exceed 
operating expenditures including transfers and depreciation by $10.22 million.  As of June 30, capital 
project expenses are at 15 percent of the total budget. Expenditures for capital projects can vary 
depending on the stage of each construction project.  Major projects to be encumbered early next fiscal 
year include the Downtown Water Replacement Project, phase 2.
