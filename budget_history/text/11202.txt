SYNOPSIS
Staff submits this fourth quarter Financial Status Report (Report) and recommends that
the City Council accept the Report and approve the budget adjustments.
BACKGROUND
The City Council adopted the FY 2021—22 budgets on June 2, 2021, with approved
General Fund revenues of $171 .17 million and expenditures of $170.14 million. The City
adopted budget for all funds is $547.9 million in revenues and $546.79 million in
expenditures.
The Report is intended to provide the City Council and the public with an overview of
the state of the City's general fiscal condition as of June 30, 2022. The Report
summarizes the preliminary General Fund major operating funds results as of June 30,
2022, and is not intended to be inclusive of all transactions. it compares budget and
actual for revenues and expenditures. The financial status of Measure X, Water Utilities,
and Harbor enterprise funds are also included.
The FY 2021-22 ended with higher than expected property tax, sales tax, and transient
occupancy tax revenues. The preliminary results for the year ending June 30, 2022
show that the General Fund will have a $1.4 million surplus.
Preliminary total revenues
are at $191.2 million and are 9 percent higher than the previous fiscal year. Total
General Fund expenditures for the fourth quarter amount to $178.4 million with a $11.4
million unrealized loss on investments. While accounting standards require the City to
report this unrealized loss, the City will not incur an actual loss, as all securities will be
held to maturity and not sold before their maturity date at market value. The unrealized
loss is primarily related to the rapid increase in interest rates, which has impacted
municipal investment portfolios nationwide. Staff continues to closely monitor revenues
and expenditures and will recommend adjustments if warranted.

Property Taxes represent 44 percent of the total General Fund revenues and are primarily received in the
December/January and April/May time frames. Property tax revenues through June 30 are at 103 percent of
the amended budget. Compared to the prior year, property tax revenue increased 5 percent. The property
tax revenue increase is consistent with the projected increase in overall assessed values of properties in
Oceanside of $1.1 billion compared to the prior year. Growth in home sales strengthened in the summer of
2020 and many cities saw an increase in properties for sale. Sales price increases in 2020 were reflected on
the 2021/22 tax rolls.
Sales Taxes represent 14 percent ofthe total annual General Fund revenues. Revenues received through June
30 are at 116 percent of the amended budget and are 4 percent higher compared to the prior year. The
increase in the current year is due to prior year decreased sales in the tourism sector and local service stations
as the Russian invasion of Ukraine resulted in higher gasoline prices. For sales occurring in the first calendar
quarter of 2022 (the most recent data available), key gains were seen in fuel and service stations, restaurants
and hotels, and county and state pools.
Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT) represent 4 percent of total General Fund revenues. The City collects 10
percent of the room rent charged by a hotel or vacation rental operator to tourists occupying a room within
the City for a period of less than 30 days. As of June 30, TOT is at 166 percent of amended budget. This is
inclusive of the TOT revenue collected by Oceanside Beach Resort (OBR), totaling $2.1M for the 'fiscal year.
Under the terms of the TOT Sharing Agreement with the developer of OBR, the developer receives 100% of
the TOT for the first three years after the hotels open. Excluding the OBR TOT revenue, TOT revenue is up 39
percent compared to prior year actuals due to decreased travel and tourism in the prior year as a result of the
COVID-19 pandemic.

ENTERPRISE FUNDS
Enterprise funds, also known as business-type activities, are used to account for the acquisition, operation
and maintenance of governmental facilities and services that are entirely or predominantly self-supporting
through user charges. The operations of Enterprise Funds are accounted for in such a manner as to show a
profit or loss similar to comparable private enterprise.
The financial status of operating funds for Water,
Sewer and Solid Waste Disposal are discussed below. Please note that Water and Sewer budgets consist of
multipie funds (operating, replacement and developer connection). Debt service is not included. Generally
accepted accounting principles (GAAP) call for fiscal analysis of Enterprise Funds to reflect a roll up of all funds
into one report.
WATER FUND REVENUES
Overall revenues for the Combined Water Funds are at 107 percent of the amended budget as ofJune 30 as
shown in Table 4. Included in Other Revenue for the year was the $2,351,414 settlement payment from the
San Diego County Water Authority for excessive rates charged by the Metropolitan Water Districts (MWD) for
the transportation of water through MWD’s pipelines during 2011-2014. This payment was not expected and
therefore was not included in the adopted budget.
Water accounts receivables have increased during COVID-19. While the majority of customers are paying their
bills in full each month, there are businesses as well as residential customers that are struggling to pay their
bills. Staff is reaching out to the delinquent customers offering reasonable payment plans to help them
become current. In an effort to help delinquent customers, the City is currently offering an Oceanside
Ratepayer Relief Program of up to a $500 credit to assist Oceanside customers that are behind on their utility
biiI. The program is available on a first-come, first-served basis to all single-family residential, multi-family,
commercial, irrigation, agriculture and recycled water customers that meet the program eligibility criteria and
requirements. The California Low Income Household Water Assistance Program (LIHWAP) is also available to
provide financial assistance to eligible Californians to help manage their residential water utility costs. This
program provides a one-time benefit (up to $2,000} on an eligible, low-income customer’s water or
wastewater bill.
