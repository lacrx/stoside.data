SYNOPSIS 
Staff submits this fourth quarter Financial Status Report (Report) and recommends that 
the City Council accept the Report and approve the budget adjustments. 
BACKGROUND 
The City Council adopted the FY 2022-23 budgets on June 8, 2022, with approved 
General Fund revenues of $188.91 million and expenditures of $186.85 million. The 
City's adopted budget for all funds is $569.79 million in revenues and $592.72 million in 
expenditures. 
The Report is intended to provide the City Council and the public with an overview of 
the state of the City's general fiscal condition as of June 30, 2023. The Report 
summarizes the preliminary General Fund major operating funds results as of June 30, 
2023, and is not intended to be inclusive of all transactions. lt compares budget and 
actual for revenues and expenditures. The financial status of Measure X, Water Utilities, 
and Harbor enterprise funds are also included. 
The FY 2022-23 ended with higher than expected property tax, sales tax, and transient 
occupancy tax revenues. The preliminary results for the year ending June 30, 2023 
show that the General Fund will have a $9.67 million surplus. 
Preliminary total 
revenues are at $212.2 million and are 9 percent higher than the previous fiscal year. 
Total General Fund expenditures for the fourth quarter amount to $202.5 million with a 
$2 million unrealized loss on investments. while accounting standards require the City 
to report this unrealized loss, the City will not incur an actual loss, as all securities will 
be held to maturity and not sold before their maturity date at market value. The 
unrealized loss is primarily related to the rapid increase in interest rates, which has 
impacted municipal investment portfolios nationwide. Staff continues to closely monitor 
revenues and expenditures and will recommend adjustments if warranted .

Property Taxes represent 39 percent of the total General Fund revenues and are primarily received in the 
December/January and April/May time frames. Property tax revenues through June 30 are at 103 percent of 
the amended budget. Compared to the prior year, property tax revenue increased 8 percent. The property 
tax revenue increase is consistent with the increase in overall assessed values of properties in Oceanside which 
increased 7.7% compared to the prior year. Growth in home sales remained strong through the summer of 
2021. Many cities saw median price increases due to low interest rates, high demand, and limited properties 
for sale. Sales price increases in 2021 were reflected on the FY 2022-23 tax rolls. The median sale price of 
Oceanside detached single-family homes from January through August 2022 was $830,000, an increase of 
$115,000 (16.1%) from the median sale price in the same period for 2021. 
Sales Taxes represent 15 percent of the total annual General Fund revenues. Revenues received through June 
30 are at 115 percent of the amended budget and are 3 percent higher compared to the prior year. The 
increase in the current year is due to the opening of a new auto transportation sector business, and increased 
revenues from local auto repair shops and restaurants and hotels. Tax receipts from fuel and service stations 
were lower compared to the prior year as the price of gasoline fell amid worry that the economy may weaken 
later this year. Along with the increases in sales tax due to the opening of a new auto transportation sector 
business, the city also paid this business $485 thousand for the year due to the sale tax abatement agreement 
the city entered into with a local developer in 2018. 
Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT) represent 8 percent of total General Fund revenues. The City collects 10 
percent of the room rent charged by a hotel or vacation rental operator to tourists occupying a room within 
the City for a period of less than 30 days. As of June 30, TOT is at 166 percent of amended budget. This is 
inclusive of the TOT revenue collected by Oceanside Beach Resort (OBR), totaling $2.9M for the fiscal year. 
Under the terms of the TOT abatement agreement with the developer of OBR, the developer receives 100% 
of the TOT for the first three years after the hotels open. TOT revenue is up 10 percent compared to prior year 
actuals due to decreased travel and tourism in the prior year as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic. 
Ambulance Billing revenues are at 128% of amended budget and a 31% increase compared to prior year. This 
increase is due to the timing of the Ground Emergency Medical Transportation (GEMT) reimbursements. 
Other Revenue & Transfers is up this year due to $1M payment from the North Review Farms Project for the 
development of a community plan for South Morro Hills, which was a condition of approval for the project. 
Use of Money & Property is up this year due to sale of Pala Road Cypress Point property for $1.0M.

ENTERPRISE FUNDS 
Enterprise funds, also known as business-type activities, are used to account for the acquisition, operation 
and maintenance of governmental facilities and services that are entirely or predominantly self-supporting 
through user charges. The operations of Enterprise Funds are accounted for in such a manner as to show a 
profit or loss similar to comparable private enterprise. The financial status of operating funds for Water, 
Sewer and Solid Waste Disposal are discussed below. Please note that Water and Sewer budgets consist of 
multiple funds (operating, replacement and developer connection). Debt service is not included. Generally 
accepted accounting principles (GAAP) call for fiscal analysis of Enterprise Funds to reflect a roll up of all funds 
into one report. 
WATER FUND REVENUES 
Overall revenues for the Combined Water Funds are at 94 percent of the amended budget as of June 30 as 
shown in Table 4. Water sales revenue was 90% of budget due to the wet year which resulted in a decrease 
in water usage. Developer fee revenue was at 146% of budget due to higher volume of meters sold than 
expected. Developer fee revenues are difficult to predict due to timing of new construction. Other revenue 
was at 162% of budget due to higher than budgeted interest earnings and proceeds from the sale of old meters 
(which were replaced by smart meters) as scrap metal. Transfers-in actuals and amended budget increased 
by $28 million due to an interfund transfer to move WIFIA funds to the capital project fund, where all of the 
project expenses were incurred. 
WATER FUND EXPENDITURES 
Table 4 highlights the amount and percentage expended by each water program. As of June 30, the Water 
Combined Funds expended 97 percent of the amended operating budget. The water admin actuals were $3 
million higher than adopted budget due less production than anticipated at the Weese and Mission Basin 
plants which resulted in higher than expected water purchases. 
As of June 30, capital project expenses are at 14 percent of the total amended budget. Expenditures for capital 
projects can vary depending on the stage of each construction project.
