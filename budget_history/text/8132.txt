Property Taxes represent 44 percent of the total General Fund revenues and are primarily received in the
December/January and April/May time frames. Property tax revenues through March 31 are at 61 percent of
the amended budget. Compared to the prior year, property tax revenue increased 6 percent. The property
tax revenue increase is consistent with the projected increase in overall assessed values of properties in
Oceanside. Continued building development and increasing housing prices are expected to continue
throughout FY 2021-22.
Sales Taxes represent 14 percent of the total annual General Fund revenues. Revenues received through
March 31 are at 76 percent of the amended budget and are 18 percent higher compared to the prior year.
The large increase in the current year is due to prior year decreased sales in the tourism sector and gasoline
stations as a result of the shelter-in-place orders resulting from the COVlD-19 pandemic. For sales occurring
in the fourth calendar quarter of 2021 (the most recent data available), key gains were seen in fuel and service
stations, restaurants and hotels, and general consumer goods.
Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT) represent 4 percent of total General Fund revenues. The City collects 10
percent of the room rent charged by a hotel or vacation rental operator to tourists occupying a room within
the City for a period of less than 30 days. As of March 31, TOT is at 93 percent of amended budget. This is
inclusive of the TOT revenue collected by Oceanside Beach Resort (OBR), totaling $1.4M for the fiscal year as
of March 31. Under the terms of the TOT Sharing Agreement with the developer of 08R, the developer
receives 100% of the TOT for the first three years after the hotels open. Excluding the OBR TOT revenue, TOT
revenue is up 43 percent compared to prior year actuals due to decreased travel and tourism in the prior year
as a result of the COVlD-19 pandemic.

GENERAL FUND EXPENDITURES
Table 2 highlights the amount and percentage expended by each department. As of March 31, the General
Fund expended 71 percent of the amended budget. The amended budget change of $14.6 million includes $2
million Council approved appropriations that were approved subsequent to the budget cut off date in addition
to continued appropriations from the prior fiscal year, as well as $12.5M of budget adjustments to the General
Fund presented in Attachment A of quarterly financial status reports.
The largest budget adjustment, of $10M, was to non-departmental due to one-time payments made to the
City’s CalPERS Unfunded Liability. Per Council Policy, we are required to use 50% of the prior year’s surplus
and 1/3 of the quarterly interest earnings to paydown debt, such as one-time payments to our CaIPERS
Unfunded Liability. Last year there was a $18.9 surplus available to the General Fund to be assigned, so 50%,
or $9.45M, went to paydown the PERS Unfunded Liability. Note that while the expense hit in the current year,
it was related to the prior year’s surplus. This is why the non-departmental CALPERS additional payment
budget and expense increased from the initial adopted budget.

ENTERPRISE FUNDS
Enterprise funds, also known as business-type activities, are used to account for the acquisition, operation
and maintenance of governmental facilities and services that are entirely or predominantly self-supporting
through user charges. The operations of Enterprise Funds are accounted for in such a manner as to show a
profit or loss similar to comparable private enterprise.
The financial status of operating funds for Water,
Sewer and Solid Waste Disposal are discussed below. Please note that Water and Sewer budgets consist of
multiple funds (operating, replacement and developer connection). Debt service is not included. Generally
accepted accounting principles (GAAP) call for fiscal analysis of Enterprise Funds to reflect a roll up of all funds
into one report.
WATER FUND REVENUES
Overall revenues for the Combined Water Funds are at 77 percent of the amended budget as of March 31 as
shown in Table 4.
Water accounts receivables have increased during COVID-19. While the majority of customers are paying their
bills in full each month, there are businesses as well as residential customers that are struggling to pay their
bills. Staff is reaching out to the delinquent customers offering reasonable payment plans to help them
become current. In an effort to help delinquent customers, the City is currently offering an Oceanside
Ratepayer Relief Program of up to a $500 credit to assist Oceanside customers that are behind on their utility
bill. The program is available on a first-come, first-served basis to all single-family residential, multi-family,
commercial, irrigation, agriculture and recycled water customers that meet the program eligibility criteria and
requirements.
